The Scoring Model

Each company is scored out of 100 using a weighted five-factor model. The point is to make comparing companies more organized and less based on gut feeling, so I look at the same five things every time. Scores above 75 are labeled Strong Model Fit, scores from 55 to 74 are labeled Mixed Model Fit, and scores below 55 are labeled Weak Model Fit. These labels only reflect how each company fits my scoring model. They are not buy or sell recommendations.

25%
Financial Strength
Higher scores go to companies with strong cash flow, manageable debt, and consistent profitability.
25%
Growth
Higher scores go to companies with rising revenue, earnings growth, or exposure to a long-term trend.
20%
Valuation
Higher scores go to companies that seem reasonably priced compared with their growth and sector.
15%
Risk
Higher scores go to companies with lower volatility, more stable demand, and fewer major uncertainties.
15%
Moat
Higher scores go to companies with strong brands, switching costs, scale, patents, or market leadership.

This is not a perfect formula. I am still learning which factors should matter most.

Model Limitations

This scoring model is simplified, and I built it as a learning tool rather than as real research. It helps me organize how I compare companies, but it has limits I try to stay honest about.

  • Some parts of the score are subjective, especially moat and risk.
  • A high score does not mean the stock will go up soon.
  • A low score does not mean the company is bad.
  • The model does not fully capture breaking news, earnings surprises, management changes, interest rates, or sudden shifts in investor sentiment.
  • The model is meant to help me ask better questions, not predict the market.

Week 3 — When a Stock You Know Goes the Other Way
June 2026
+$28.65 (+0.29%)

Portfolio movement: The simulator was up around 0.29% this week, which was actually better than it sounds because it happened while some of my biggest names were down. AAPL dropped around 0.72% and MSFT dropped around 1.18%, but 8 out of 15 stocks were still up on the day. That balance felt different from earlier weeks where one or two stocks were doing all the work.

Biggest surprise: SMCI dropped 8.10% this week after jumping 15.66% the week before. Honestly I expected it. After Week 2 I understood that a high beta stock can swing that hard in either direction, so seeing it reverse was not shocking. But expected and comfortable are two different things. SMCI is a stock I actually owned with my dad, so watching it drop after watching it jump brought up a different kind of feeling than just reading a number on a screen.

Concept I learned: The portfolio being up while AAPL and MSFT were down was a useful reminder of what diversification actually does. I picked 15 companies across different sectors partly for this reason, but I had not really seen it play out clearly until this week. The smaller names and healthcare names held things up while the big tech names pulled back. That is the practical version of what I had been reading about in theory.

Next question: I am still thinking about how to separate the emotional side from the analytical side when it comes to SMCI specifically. Because I have a personal history with that stock, I notice I pay more attention to it than the others. I am not sure if that is a good thing or a bias I need to be careful about. Real investors talk about this too, the idea that familiarity with a company can make you overconfident or overly attached.

Week 2 — Scores vs. Price Action
June 2026
+$15.44 (+0.15%)

Portfolio movement: The simulator was almost flat this week, up around 0.15%. The total number mattered less to me than how differently the individual stocks moved.

Biggest surprise: MSFT has my highest model score at 86/100, but it was actually down about 3.18% on the day, while SMCI jumped around 15.66%, the biggest move on my whole list. At first I thought it was weird that Microsoft could be my top-scored stock and still drop that much.

Concept I learned: My score is a long-term read on company quality, not a prediction of short-term price. A stock can move just from circulating news, an analyst comment, or money rotating between sectors, none of which changes the actual business. I had to look up beta and market cap before SMCI's jump made sense to me. A beta around 1.95 means SMCI tends to move nearly twice as much as the market, and a smaller market cap means it takes less money to swing the price. As someone who used to own this stock, I had already seen this kind of move before.

Next question: I want to figure out whether my model gives too much weight to growth and not enough to valuation, since some of my high-growth names still score well even when they look expensive.

Week 1 — Project Launch
June 2026
Starting point

Before this project I honestly thought finance was mostly just buying stocks and trying to pick the right company before everyone else did. I did not think much about the deeper stuff like revenue, business model, competition, or whether the price actually makes sense for what the company is. Building this made me actually look at that.

Researching MSFT, NVDA, and KO showed me how different "big stock" can mean. Microsoft felt huge and stable with a bunch of different ways to make money: software, cloud, AI. NVIDIA was more exciting but also more confusing because so much of its price is based on what people think AI will become, not just what is happening right now. Coca-Cola is not trying to grow fast at all, it just has a brand and demand that does not go away. Comparing them made me realize how much of a stock price is a bet on the future and not just a reflection of the present.

The most personal part of this whole project is SMCI being on the growth list. My dad and I actually bought it in August 2020 because we thought servers and data centers were going to matter more as computing demand grew. We sold in early 2023 after it had already gone up a lot. We figured it was smarter to take the win than get greedy. It kept climbing after we sold, which stings a little, but I do not think that makes it a bad decision. We made a reasonable call with the information we had at the time. That is the lesson I keep coming back to with this project. Investing is not about timing things perfectly, it is about making a reasonable decision and being okay with not knowing how it plays out.

MSFT
Microsoft Corporation · Core Company
Strong Model Fit
Why I Included It

Microsoft has several separate businesses that would each be large companies on their own: Azure (cloud), Office 365 (software), and LinkedIn and gaming. When one part slows down, the others can help make up for it. Before this project, I mostly thought of Microsoft as Windows, Office, and Xbox. Researching it made me realize how important recurring software revenue and cloud computing are to the company.

What I Think the Business Case Is

A lot of Microsoft's revenue is now subscription based, which means it is recurring and more predictable than a one-time sale. Investors tend to value steady, recurring revenue more highly because it is more reliable. Microsoft has also been adding AI features through Copilot across its products, which could be a long-term advantage given how widely its software is already used. Azure has recently been growing quickly, which made me realize how important cloud revenue is to the company.

What Could Go Wrong

Valuation is the main thing I worry about. If cloud growth slows, the stock could fall even if the business is still solid. There is also regulatory pressure in Europe and growing competition from Google Cloud and Amazon in the cloud space.

What I Learned Researching This

I did not really understand the difference between one-time product revenue and subscription revenue before this project. Microsoft's move from selling Office as a one-time purchase to charging monthly changed the whole character of its financials and made earnings more predictable. That is a lesson that applies well beyond just Microsoft.

NVDA
NVIDIA Corporation · Higher-Risk Growth Company
Mixed Model Fit
Why I Included It

NVIDIA's beta is high (around 2.2), which means it tends to move a lot more than the overall market. I did not include it just because it is volatile though. NVIDIA stood out to me because its chips are central to many AI systems, and demand for them has been very strong.

What I Think the Business Case Is

NVIDIA does not just sell hardware. I learned that its CUDA software ecosystem, which it has built up over many years, makes it harder for customers to switch away to a competitor. Big cloud companies like Amazon, Google, Microsoft, and Meta have been spending heavily on its chips to build AI capacity. Competitors like AMD are trying to catch up, but software ecosystems take a long time to build.

What Could Go Wrong

The valuation is the biggest risk. NVIDIA's AI chip demand has been extremely strong, which is one reason investors have valued the company so highly. If that spending slows down, or if companies decide they have built enough capacity, revenue could drop quickly. Export restrictions to China also cut off part of its market.

What I Learned Researching This

I learned the difference between a cyclical business and a longer-term growth story. Semiconductors used to be very cyclical, rising and falling with PC and phone cycles. NVIDIA's AI exposure may have changed that, but I am not sure, and that uncertainty is part of why I treat it as a higher-risk growth company rather than a core company.

KO
The Coca-Cola Company · Core Company
Strong Model Fit
Why I Included It

Coca-Cola's beta is low (around 0.35), which means it usually moves less than the overall market. It generally moves less than high-growth technology stocks, which makes it a more defensive holding. I wanted at least one company like this on my list to balance out the riskier names.

What I Think the Business Case Is

Coca-Cola helped me understand what a moat means in a simple way. Even though soda is not a complicated product, the brand, the distribution network in over 200 countries, and customer habits make the company difficult to compete with. It also sells hundreds of other drinks beyond cola, and it has a long history of paying and raising its dividend.

What Could Go Wrong

Health trends are a long-term concern, since people are shifting toward lower-sugar drinks and Coca-Cola has been slower to adapt in some markets. Currency risk also matters, because a strong dollar can lower its reported earnings even when the underlying business is doing fine.

What I Learned Researching This

Researching KO taught me what a Dividend Aristocrat actually means, which is a company that has raised its dividend for at least 25 years in a row. KO has done it for over 60. That kind of consistency is only possible with very stable cash flow, which is itself a sign of a strong, durable business.

SMCI
Super Micro Computer · Higher-Risk Growth Company
Mixed Model Fit
Why This One's Personal

My dad and I actually bought SMCI in August 2020. It was not my idea at first. My dad helped me think through the business: SMCI builds high-density servers for data centers, and he thought demand for that kind of computing power was going to grow. I did not fully understand every detail at the time, but the basic logic made sense to me.

What Happened

We bought in at around $30 a share and sold in early 2023 at around $92, based on the prices I have checked. We sold because the stock had already gone up a lot and it felt smarter to lock in the gain than keep pushing our luck. It kept climbing after we sold, which is a little frustrating to look back on, but I do not think that makes it a bad decision. A profitable choice can still feel imperfect in hindsight.

Why I Included It

SMCI connects to the same AI trend as some of my other picks, since companies building AI systems need a lot of servers. It has also been volatile partly because of past accounting questions, which is one reason I classify it as a higher-risk growth company rather than a core company.

What I Learned Researching This

This is the experience that taught me investing is not about timing the exact top or bottom. We made a reasonable decision with what we knew, and it worked out, but the point is not that we picked a winner. The point is that I saw how hard these decisions are, how much uncertainty is involved, and how emotions like the fear of missing out or the fear of giving back gains affect the choice.

What I've Learned Building This

01
Finance is portfolio construction, not just stock picking

I started thinking this project was about finding good companies. It turned out to be more about building a system: how much risk to take, how to balance stability and growth, and why spreading across sectors matters more than any single pick.

02
Beta changed how I think about risk

Before this project, I mostly thought risk meant "could this company go bankrupt." Beta taught me that risk also means how much a stock amplifies market moves, and that a list full of high-beta stocks can get hurt badly in a downturn even if every company in it is solid.

03
Valuation is harder than it looks

Scoring NVIDIA's valuation made me realize how much uncertainty is built into a high P/E stock. You are not just paying for what the company is, you are paying for what you believe it will become, and that is a different kind of judgment.

04
Consistency matters more than any single call

The most useful part of this project is not the website itself, it is the habit of checking prices, reading about the companies, and updating the journal each week. Steady, small decisions over time seem to matter more than one big idea.

◆ Core Company Selection Criteria

  • Lower beta, so it tends to move less than the overall market
  • Large, well-established company
  • Consistent free cash flow over several years
  • Dividend history or steady buybacks
  • A clear leadership position in its industry
  • Reasonable debt compared with similar companies

◆ Higher-Risk Growth Selection Criteria

  • A large market it could keep expanding into
  • Faster revenue growth than the core names
  • Exposure to a long-term trend such as AI or quantum computing
  • Higher beta accepted, since more volatility comes with the upside
  • Often smaller, with more room to grow but more risk
  • Kept to a smaller position size because of that risk
