Week 1 — Project Launch
June 2026
Initializing…

Before this project I honestly thought finance was mostly just buying stocks and trying to pick the right company before everyone else did. I didn't think much about the deeper stuff like revenue, business model, competition, whether the price actually makes sense for what the company is. Building this made me actually look at that.

Researching MSFT, NVDA, and KO showed me how different "big stock" can mean. Microsoft felt huge and stable with a bunch of different ways to make money: software, cloud, AI. NVIDIA was more exciting but also more confusing because so much of its price is based on what people think AI will become, not just what's happening right now. Coca-Cola isn't trying to grow fast at all, it just has a brand and demand that doesn't go away. Comparing them made me realize how much of a stock price is a bet on the future and not just a reflection of the present.

The most personal part of this whole project is SMCI being on the growth list. My dad and I actually bought it in August 2020 because we thought servers and data centers were going to matter more as computing demand grew. We sold in early 2023 after it had already gone up a lot. Figured it was smarter to take the win than get greedy. It kept climbing after we sold which stings a little but I don't think that makes it a bad decision. We made a good call with the information we had at the time. That's the lesson I keep coming back to with this project. Investing isn't about timing things perfectly, it's about making a reasonable decision and being okay with not knowing how it plays out.

MSFT
Microsoft Corporation · Core Holding
BUY
Why It's in the Core Tier

Microsoft has three separate businesses that would each be large companies on their own: Azure (cloud), Office 365 (productivity software), and LinkedIn/gaming. This diversification is rare. When one segment slows, the others tend to compensate. Azure's revenue has been growing above 25% year-over-year, which is exceptional for a company this size.

The Investment Thesis

Microsoft's shift to subscription-based revenue (Azure, Office 365, Xbox Game Pass) means revenue is recurring and predictable — the opposite of a one-time sale model. Recurring revenue is valued at a premium by the market because it's more reliable. The integration of OpenAI's technology into Copilot across all Microsoft products gives it an AI distribution advantage that will take years for competitors to replicate at the same scale.

Key Risks

Valuation is the main risk. A P/E above 35 means the market is pricing in continued strong growth — if Azure slows, the multiple could compress quickly. Regulatory pressure in Europe around Teams bundling is an ongoing concern. Competition from Google Cloud and AWS in the cloud segment is intensifying.

What I Learned Researching This

I didn't understand the difference between product revenue and subscription revenue before this project. Microsoft's move from selling Office as a one-time purchase to charging monthly changed the entire character of its financials — it made earnings more predictable, which reduces risk and supports a higher valuation multiple. That's a lesson that applies far beyond just Microsoft.

NVDA
NVIDIA Corporation · Growth Bet
WATCH
Why It's in the Growth Tier

NVIDIA's beta above 1.8 immediately flags it as volatile — it moves significantly more than the market. But the reason it belongs here isn't just the volatility, it's the nature of the business. GPU demand for AI training is currently growing faster than NVIDIA can manufacture chips. That's a rare supply constraint problem, which usually means pricing power and strong margins.

The Investment Thesis

NVIDIA is not just a chip company anymore — it's the infrastructure layer of the AI economy. Every major cloud provider (Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Meta) is spending billions on H100 and Blackwell GPUs to build AI capacity. NVIDIA's CUDA software platform, built over 15 years, creates a switching cost moat that is genuinely hard to replicate. AMD is trying, but software ecosystems don't get built overnight.

Key Risks

The valuation is the elephant in the room. A P/E above 50 means the market expects NVIDIA to keep growing at a rate that would be extraordinary for any company. If AI capex spending slows — if companies decide they've built enough GPU capacity — NVIDIA's revenue could drop sharply. China export restrictions also cut off a meaningful revenue opportunity.

What I Learned Researching This

I learned the difference between a cyclical business and a secular growth story. Semiconductors used to be deeply cyclical — demand surged and crashed with PC and smartphone cycles. NVIDIA's AI exposure may have structurally changed that, but I'm not certain. That uncertainty is why it's a Growth Bet at 5% allocation rather than a Core Holding at 10%.

KO
The Coca-Cola Company · Core Holding
BUY
Why It's in the Core Tier

Beta of 0.61 — Coca-Cola barely moves when the market moves. That's the definition of a defensive stock. In 2022, when the S&P 500 fell over 18%, KO dropped less than 3%. That kind of stability has a real portfolio value, especially when paired with more volatile positions.

The Investment Thesis

Warren Buffett has owned Coca-Cola since 1988 and still holds it. His reasoning is simple: the brand is worth more than any individual product. Coke has distribution in over 200 countries, a portfolio of 500+ beverages beyond just cola, and pricing power — they can raise prices modestly without losing significant volume because the brand loyalty is that strong. That's a textbook economic moat.

Key Risks

Health trends are the long-term headwind. Consumer preferences are shifting toward low-sugar and functional beverages, and KO has been slower to adapt than rivals in some markets. Currency risk is also real — with revenue in 200 countries, a strong dollar suppresses reported earnings even when the business itself is doing well.

What I Learned Researching This

Researching KO taught me what a Dividend Aristocrat actually means — it's a company that has raised its dividend for 25+ consecutive years. KO has done it for over 60. That consistency is only possible with very stable free cash flow, which is itself a signal of competitive moat. High, stable dividends are a form of proof, not just an income feature.

Finance Major · Community College → Transfer Candidate

I built this project to close the gap between studying finance and actually doing it. My goal is to transfer into a four-year university as a Finance major, and I wanted something concrete to show — not just grades and coursework, but evidence that I understand how markets work and can build analytical frameworks to make sense of them. This watchlist is that evidence. Every pick has a thesis, every score has a methodology, and every journal entry is an honest account of what the data did versus what I expected.

What I've Learned Building This

01
Finance is portfolio construction, not stock picking

I started thinking this project was about finding good companies. It turned out to be about building a system — how much risk to take, how to balance stability and growth, and why diversification across sectors matters more than any individual pick.

02
Beta changed how I think about risk

Before this project, I thought risk meant "could this company go bankrupt." Beta taught me that risk also means how much a stock amplifies market moves — and that a portfolio full of high-beta stocks gets hurt badly in downturns even if every company in it is fundamentally solid.

03
Valuation is a humbling exercise

Scoring NVIDIA's valuation made me realize how much uncertainty is baked into a high P/E stock. You're not just paying for what the company is — you're paying for what you believe it will become. That's a different kind of judgment, and it's harder than it looks.

04
Consistency matters more than any single insight

The most valuable part of this project isn't the website — it's the habit of checking prices, reading earnings summaries, and updating the journal every week. Real portfolio managers don't make one brilliant call; they make thousands of small, disciplined decisions over time.

◆ Core Selection Criteria

  • Beta below 1.10 — moves less than the overall market
  • Market cap above $100B — size provides stability
  • Positive free cash flow for 5+ consecutive years
  • Dividend history or consistent buyback program
  • Industry leadership with defensible competitive moat
  • Low debt-to-equity relative to sector peers

◆ Growth Selection Criteria

  • High total addressable market with early penetration
  • Revenue growth rate above 20% year-over-year
  • Structural tailwind — AI, quantum, energy drinks, etc.
  • High beta accepted — volatility is the price of upside
  • Smaller or mid-cap with room to grow significantly
  • Position sizing discipline — max 5% per growth bet
